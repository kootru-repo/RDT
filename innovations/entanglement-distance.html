I. The Entanglement Distance Problem

1.1 The Standard QM View

In standard quantum mechanics, entanglement has no fundamental distance limit. Two particles can be entangled regardless of their spatial separation — whether nanometers or light-years apart. The correlation is "spooky action at a distance" with no mechanism and no maximum range.

1.2 The RDT Prediction

RDT makes a novel, testable prediction: there exists a maximum Ω-distance dmax beyond which entanglement is impossible:

$$d_\Omega(\sigma_A, \sigma_B) > d_{max} \implies \text{No entanglement possible}$$

This isn't about physical spatial distance — it's about distance in configuration space Ω. Two particles can be spatially far apart but Ω-adjacent (and thus entangled), or spatially close but Ω-distant (and thus unable to entangle).

2.2 The Heat Kernel Connection

From our HIGH-03 resolution, traversal through Ω is diffusion governed by the Fokker-Planck equation:

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial \tau} = D\nabla^2\rho - \nabla \cdot (\rho \nabla S / T)$$

The heat kernel K(σ, σ'; τ) describes how probability spreads from σ to σ' in time τ:

$$K(\sigma, \sigma'; \tau) \approx (4\pi D\tau)^{-n/2} \exp\left(-\frac{d_\Omega^2}{4D\tau}\right)$$

This kernel directly measures correlation strength: K → 1 means perfect correlation (entanglement), K → 0 means no correlation.

III. Heat Kernel Coherence Threshold

3.1 The Coherence Condition

For quantum entanglement to exist, the correlation amplitude must exceed a minimum threshold:

$$K(\sigma_A, \sigma_B; \tau) \geq K_{min}$$

What is Kmin? From quantum mechanics, the minimum distinguishable amplitude is set by the total number of configurations:

F1: |Ω| = N (finite totality)
Distinguishability: Kmin ~ 1/N (single configuration distinguishable from N total)
Maximum: Kmax = 1 (perfect overlap)

3.2 Setting the Threshold

From the heat kernel formula:

$$K = (4\pi D\tau)^{-n/2} \exp\left(-\frac{d^2}{4D\tau}\right)$$

At the threshold K = Kmin = 1/N:

$$\exp\left(-\frac{d_{max}^2}{4D\tau}\right) = \frac{(4\pi D\tau)^{n/2}}{N}$$

Taking logarithms:

$$-\frac{d_{max}^2}{4D\tau} = \frac{n}{2}\ln(4\pi D\tau) - \ln N$$

For large N (cosmological Ω), the ln N term dominates:

Maximum Entanglement Distance

$$d_{max} = 2\sqrt{D\tau \ln N}$$

Where:

  • D = Ω-diffusion coefficient
  • τ = available correlation time
  • N = |Ω| = total configurations
  • ln N ~ Suniverse/kB (total entropy)

Laser pulse: Initiates diffusion trajectory through Ω

4.3 Extracting the Diffusion Coefficient

The diffusion time formula:

$$\tau_{entangle} = \frac{d_\Omega^2}{4D}$$

For atomic-scale systems, the characteristic Ω-distance is approximately the Bohr radius:

$$d \sim a_0 \approx 5.3 \times 10^{-11} \text{ m}$$

Solving for D:

$$D = \frac{d^2}{4\tau} = \frac{(5.3 \times 10^{-11})^2}{4 \times 232 \times 10^{-18}} \approx 3 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^2/\text{s}$$

Ω-Diffusion Coefficient (Atomic Scale)

$$D_{atomic} \approx 10^{-6} \text{ m}^2/\text{s}$$

This is the first experimental constraint on diffusion in configuration space, derived from the TU Wien entanglement formation measurement.

4.4 The "Superposition of Birth Times"

The TU Wien researchers noted that the escaping electron doesn't have a precise "birth time" — it exists in a superposition of having left earlier or later.

In RDT, this is natural: the traversal through Ω is governed by the heat kernel K, which gives probability amplitudes, not sharp trajectories. The 232 as is the expectation value of the diffusion time, with quantum uncertainty around it.

The practical limit on entanglement isn't dmax itself, but the available time τ for correlation to develop:

$$d_{practical} = 2\sqrt{D\tau_{available}}$$

For most laboratory experiments with τ ~ seconds, dpractical ~ mm scale. The fundamental dmax only becomes relevant at cosmological scales or for systems with vastly different Ω-topology.

VII. The π Connection

7.1 π in the Heat Kernel

The heat kernel normalization contains explicit π dependence:

$$K(\sigma, \sigma'; \tau) = \frac{1}{(4\pi D\tau)^{n/2}} \exp\left(-\frac{d^2}{4D\tau}\right)$$

The 4π in the denominator comes from the Gaussian integral:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi}$$

In n dimensions: (4πDτ)n/2 ensures probability conservation.

7.2 Connection to κ = 1/(4π)

From the π-derivations document, the coupling constant κ = 1/(4π) arises from the same Gaussian normalization. The dmax formula inherits this structure:

$$d_{max}^2 = 4D\tau \ln N = 4D\tau \ln N \times \underbrace{\frac{4\pi}{4\pi}}_{\text{geometric factor}}$$

The factor 4 in "4Dτ" is the same 4 that appears in Varadhan's theorem and the heat kernel — it's not arbitrary but geometric.
