Symmetry Forces L = f(v²)

Landau & Lifshitz, Mechanics (1960)
Symmetry requirements constrain the Lagrangian

The Argument

  • Homogeneity of space: L cannot depend on absolute position
  • Isotropy of space: L can only depend on |v|, not direction → L = f(v²)
  • Galilean invariance: Under boost v → v + ε, invariance requires ∂L/∂(v²) = const

Result

Symmetry alone forces: $L = \frac{m}{2}v^2 = \frac{m}{2}|\dot{\gamma}|_d^2$

Higher Powers Are Forbidden

Ostrogradsky's Theorem (1850)
Higher-derivative theories have unbounded energy

Ostrogradsky's Theorem (1850)

Any Lagrangian with time derivatives higher than first order (and non-degenerate in the highest derivative) leads to a Hamiltonian that is unbounded below. The system has no stable ground state.

A quartic term L ∝ v⁴ produces fourth-order equations of motion, triggering Ostrogradsky instability. Energy can become arbitrarily negative — the vacuum is unstable.

Result

Stability forbids v⁴, v⁶, and all higher powers.

3

Odd Powers Are Non-Smooth

Mathematical Analysis
Odd powers have cusps — not differentiable at the origin

For a velocity vector v, the magnitude |v| = √(v²) is not differentiable at v = 0. The gradient ∇|v| = v/|v| is undefined at the origin.

Similarly, all odd powers |v|ⁿ with n odd have cusps. First-order equations from L ∝ |v| also violate time-reversal symmetry.

Result

Smoothness forbids |v|, |v|³, and all odd powers.
